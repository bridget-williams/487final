What's the big deal about Coral?

While coral reefs take up only 1% of the world's ocean space, they contain over 25% of the ocean's species. And, they're disappearing at an alarming rate. If this continues, 60% of original reefs will be gone within 30 years.

But you probably already knew that.

Here’s why, and why it matters.

What exactly is a Coral?

A coral, as many people know them, is actually a combination of a bunch of two different species of living animals. A singular coral itself is called a polyp and it looks like a tiny sea anemone (Corals and Anemones are actually closely related). It has algae, the zooxanthellae, that lives inside its tissues. These two species provide each other things they need to survive. This is why the relationship is called symbiotic, and the algae is often called the symbiot. The coral provides a safe, stable home, as well as carbon dioxide for the algae. The algae produces food for the coral through photosynthesis.

Why Coral is in trouble

There are two major problems facing corals today, and they go hand-in-hand. Increasing Carbon Dioxide levels in the atmosphere is the root problem. This increased CO2, causes acidification of the ocean water when it dissolves as well as traps heat in the atmosphere, warming the ocean temperature. Both of these cause trouble for coral. When coral gets distressed it expels its algae. This what is called bleaching. Contrary to popular belief, coral bleaching does not mean coral death. It’s more like coral sickness in that if a coral does not reabsorb the algae after bleaching, it will eventually die.

It's not as simple as it seems

Although bleaching is troubling and becoming more widespread as ocean acidification and warming continues, there is some hope for corals. First, there are many other factors that influence the health of a coral beyond the algae. Researchers are still finding other things, like Fungi and Bacteria that also influence their lives. Also, researchers are finding that while both acidification and warming present problems, warming presents a larger more imminent danger to corals. Finally, there is some hope for corals, even as warming and acidification continue. There have been some corals found that are resistant to bleaching under extreme temperatures, and because of the way coral spawning works, they might be able to spread that heat resistant gene to create entire colonies or reefs of heat resistant corals.

Coral in North Carolina?

North Carolina as a Case Study

While North Carolina might not be what you picture when you think of coral reefs, it is home to over 50 artificial reef environments. Many of these formed on shipwrecks off of the coast, nicknamed "The Graveyard of the Atlantic." These reefs play a crucial role in local environments and economies.

Across the world, coral reefs are important for a number of reasons.

First, they are a keystone in one of the most diverse ecosystem types on the planet. Not only are corals alive themselves, but as they grow, they create habitat for many other marine species.

Because coral reefs are home to so many fish species, they have also become very important to the economy of the land around them.
